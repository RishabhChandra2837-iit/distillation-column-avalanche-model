This project investigates how disturbances propagate within a distillation column, a critical unit used in chemical processing industries. The distillation column is modeled as a complex interconnected system consisting of multiple trays, each influencing its neighboring trays. Using principles from complexity science, the study explores whether sudden large-scale flooding events can arise naturally from the system’s internal dynamics rather than from isolated equipment failures. A Python-based simulation was developed to model liquid accumulation on each tray and the overflow behavior when a tray exceeds its flooding threshold. These overflows can trigger cascading chain reactions, referred to as avalanches, across the column. The simulation demonstrates that small disturbances can sometimes lead to disproportionately large system-wide upsets. Analysis of the results reveals that these avalanche events follow a power-law distribution, a hallmark of self-organized criticality observed in many natural complex systems. The study also shows that larger columns with more trays are more susceptible to severe cascade events. Overall, the project provides a new complexity-science perspective on distillation column behavior and offers insights into process safety, control, and the intrinsic dynamics of industrial chemical systems.
